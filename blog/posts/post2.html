Lessons from Collaborative Robotics

May 15, 2025

Our senior design team got paired with B&G Manufacturing, a machine shop that produces bolts on CNC lathes. The problem they handed us was straightforward on the surface: a worker was spending their entire shift manually loading and unloading parts into the lathe, the threading machine, and the inspection station. Repetitive, physically demanding, and not a great use of a skilled machinist. They wanted it automated — but on a budget, and without shutting down the floor to install a traditional industrial robot cell.

That last constraint was the key one. A traditional industrial robot is fast and powerful, but it needs a safety cage because it genuinely doesn't know if a person is in its path. That cage takes up floor space, disrupts the workflow around it, and costs money. A collaborative robot — a cobot — is designed differently. It has force/torque sensing that causes it to stop immediately on unexpected contact, which means it can work right alongside people without a cage. That's what made this feasible in their environment.

The end-effector is where the real work is

We were using a Universal Robots arm, which is a well-proven platform. But here's the thing — the robot arm itself is almost a solved problem at this point. What nobody sells you off the shelf is an end-effector designed for your specific part geometry, at your specific grip force, within the robot's payload limit, that won't damage the part. That's the actual engineering problem, and that's where we spent most of our time.

We went through three design iterations in SolidWorks before settling on a gripper design we were confident in. One thing that isn't obvious until you're doing it: the center of mass of the end-effector plus the part it's holding matters a lot. An offset center of mass creates a moment on the robot's wrist joint that shows up as positioning error and wear over time. We caught that early in CAD and adjusted the geometry before we ever printed anything, which saved us a headache later.

We also integrated a vibratory feeder bowl to orient and present parts to the robot consistently — because a cobot can't pick up a randomly tumbled bolt. The whole system is: feeder bowl → robot picks part → loads lathe → transfers to threader → drops at inspection. Every station had to talk to the next one.

ANSYS caught something we missed

Before we finalized the end-effector design, we ran static and fatigue analysis in ANSYS Workbench. The critical load case was a sudden stop — if the cobot hits its force threshold and halts abruptly mid-motion, the end-effector takes a significant impulse load. The FEA flagged a stress concentration at one of the mounting bracket corners that we hadn't caught visually. A fillet radius increase fixed it, redistributing the stress across a larger area. Small change, big difference in the fatigue life numbers.

We also did FMEA — Failure Mode and Effects Analysis — which meant systematically going through every component and asking "what if this fails, and what happens next?" Some of it felt like overkill at the time. Then one of the machinists on the floor mentioned an edge case where parts occasionally come off the lathe slightly out of spec and mentioned that our gripper needed to handle that gracefully. We had it in the FMEA. We'd already accounted for it. That felt good.

The numbers

After testing, the system reduced part cycle time from 9.4 minutes down to 5.5 minutes — over a 40% improvement. Total project cost came in under $75,000, which is significantly below the $100K–$250K range for comparable industry solutions. The return on investment works out to roughly one year, which is what the sponsor was looking for.

The manual handling reduction was also the part I was most personally glad about. The machinist who had been doing that task didn't lose their job — they got moved to higher-judgment work that the robot genuinely can't do. That's the outcome you want from automation. Not fewer people, better-used people.

What I'd tell someone starting this kind of project

Talk to the operators before you finalize anything. We caught two failure modes we hadn't considered because someone on the floor told us how parts actually behave in practice, as opposed to how they behave in the spec sheet. No simulation or CAD model captures that knowledge. It's just in the room, if you ask.

Also: budget extra time for integration. Every individual piece of our system worked fine in isolation. The first day we ran the full sequence end-to-end on the actual floor, several things did not work. That's normal, but plan for it. Integration is its own engineering problem and it will take longer than you think.
